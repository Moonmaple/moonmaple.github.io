Interest in IT

I never realised that I had an interest in IT.  While I was growing up in the 1980’s, my father ran a successful software services company. 

This meant that we always had computers in the house, and he would sit in front of a DOS screen for hours.  As far as I was aware, every house had this! 

Loading games via 25 floppy disks just to fly an x-wing fighter in 16 VGA colour was normal, right?

When I got to school in the mid 80’s, I didn’t understand why there was only one computer for the whole school.  And who was Carmen Santiago?

By the time I left school, my computer skills meant that my first job in 1997 was a 1st Level Helpdesk Support role. 

It was during this work experience that I realised, not everyone had the computer skills that I had.  Not everyone grew up with computer parts lying around the house that you could build a computer from.

I worked in desktop, software and printer support for about 8 years (going between coordination, hardware management and small business support).  But I never “loved” the work. 

 

But then I discovered Data Administration and Management.  It wasn’t the same installation and support process day in and day out like IT support. 

I was making sure that business data was accurate, working and able to be used for analysis.  Being able to make data “clean” and usable really made me feel like I had achieved something.

I discovered that what made me really excited was a clean process, a clean data set and valid insights from the usage of that data.

 

I’ve come to RMIT through Open Universities.  RMIT has a great reputation locally here in Melbourne.  When I enrolled in the first unit, I discovered my Dad had studied at RMIT 30 years ago!

This is my second unit through RMIT.  I am hoping to do down the path of Data Analytics.  I want to work with big data sets and use predictive modelling to produce interesting insights.

Aptitude and Personality Tests

I’m pretty hesitant about online personality and aptitude tests.  It’s very hard to determine how someone learns or listens when it is only being done via an online interface.  Especially if they are free!

But personality testing is big business.  A lot of job interview processes that I have been through required the Myers Briggs test. 

There are all kinds of articles about why this test is not all that relevant. 

These articles talk about how the mother and daughter who developed it were not trained psychologists, the results put people’s personality into binary categorisations and it doesn’t allow for the fluidity and complexity of people and their experiences. The Jung theories it is based on, were even questioned by Jung!

Every time I do the Myers Briggs, I get a different result.  I am introverted, until I need to be when collaborating or socialising.  But I always get an introverted score. 

Is that because I reason things out myself before sound boarding it on someone? Who knows? But I really feel that Myers Briggs is an overblown, over rated, ridiculous way of finding out about someone.

An interesting opinion on Myers Briggs; https://www.bbc.com/news/blogs-echochambers-28315137

 

My attitude toward personality tests means that I would not take their results into account if I was forming a team.

However, if someone was really excited about personality tests, I would take that into account! It would be a solid no from me.

Project Idea

I have recently been involved in an experience with local council. I have a Beagle.  Beagles like to bark. A lot. They also like to howl. 

We have worked very hard on training our dog to not bark all the time. 

We had direct reaction and redirection to her every time she barked.  We created obstacles throughout the yard so that she could not get to the parts of the boundary where she liked to bark.

We also introduced a citronella collar that would spray her every time she barked.  It was a long process, but by the time she was 18 months to 2 years old, our efforts paid off. 

We now have a dog that still barks, but not consistently and not causing any affect to our neighbours.  She is pretty well behaved.

 

However, a neighbour got a new dog.  That barks.  All day, all night.  So much so we feel terrible for them. 

Every now and then, our dog responds to the neighbour’s dog barking.  Other neighbours have reported this neighbour to the council. 

One of the responses from the barking dog neighbour to the council was that our dog provoked their dog into barking.

We were approached by Council and I knew this was not the case.  But how on earth could I prove it? 

I would start to video when the neighbours dog was barking (which there was ample opportunity), and there would be my dog in the video, doing her thing, but not barking. 

Despite these videos this evidence did not lend any credibility to my argument that it was not my dog. It was argued that I only went out to video when my dog was not barking.

So, we were asked by the council to provide a “diary” of the barking.  A diary of both our dog and the neighbour’s dog.

Every time my dog barked or the neighbour’s dog barked, I had to note it in the diary.  Council then uses these diaries to see if they “match” with another neighbour that was also keeping a diary.

 

This approach to data collection was obviously not going to be consistent or accurate. 

People aren’t home all the time, people can fill out the diary when they feel like it.  Or they can falsify the information they were noting in the diary.

 

What if there was a way for council to know that the barking was being tracked correctly and how often a dog barks?  What if they could know if the information was being collected consistently?

How about a collar that collects the data and reports it via an app! 

It registers when a dog is barking and it tracks the time, the date and how much barking there was. 

It also tracks the time, date and length that the bark collar was turned on and on a dog because it can recognise temperature and therefore know that it is on a dog’s neck.

This data can be collected and used by the dog owner and provided to council. 

No more hand diaries, no more she said, he said, and they can issue nuisance dog fines, or dismiss neighbours allegations with accurate data! 

 

This could also be used for dog training.  We never knew how much our dog was barking when we were not home. 

This app would tell you the problem times so that you can customise your training. 

It could also be integrated with a citronella collar which can let the dog know that there is a negative action every time they bark.

 

There would need to be a collar that was fitted out with a sensors (there are currently collars on the market that already have this) and a Bluetooth antenna.

The collar would need to have memory in the collar in order to store the data.  Then there would be an app to transfer the data to and collate and report.

The collar would sync with Bluetooth like a fit bit and transfer the data. 

Someone with software skills would need to write the data collection app.  This app will have a report output function to provide to council or the owner.   

The collar would be custom made, so someone with hardware knowledge is going to be required. The collar would contain things like:

·         Battery

·         Waterproof Contacts

·         USB Charger port

·         Bluetooth Antenna

·         Temp Sensor

·         Bark Sensor

·         Motherboard

·         Microcontroller

·         Memory

 

Fitbits and Watches already have most of this hardware built into them. (I fix it pulled one apart so you can see what is in it: https://www.ifixit.com/Teardown/Fitbit+Flex+Teardown/16050)

So it is entirely possible to build something like this, but someone with a high degree of hardware knowledge would be required. 

Someone with electrical engineering experience or someone who might be able to use an existing hardware technology and customise to our needs.

Having a collar that knows how long it has been on a dog, when the dog barked and how often would mean that objective data can be provided to determine the level of nuisance without wondering if people are filling out data correctly.
